01 // WHY THIS EXISTS
■ OUT OF WORLD — ALL PARTICIPANTS
THIS GAME GOES PLACES

SPIKE: The Reeves Trials is a game about psychological unraveling, grief, loss of control, and what happens when people are denied their own emotional lives. It is also a game about care, loyalty, and what it means to be human. Both of those things are true simultaneously, and holding both is the work.

The content in this campaign can include: body horror (mild), dissociation and loss of self, grief and loss, psychological manipulation, institutionalized control, identity and memory, and the experience of emotions that have no name or framework. None of this is presented gratuitously — it all serves the story. But it is real, and some of it may land harder than expected for some people at some times.

The tools in this document are not a sign that the game is dangerous. They are a sign that the people running it care enough to build in ways to navigate it well. Use them. That's what they're for.

THE REAL-WORLD PRINCIPLE

The fiction exists to serve the people at the table — not the other way around. No story beat, dramatic moment, or narrative payoff is worth genuine distress for a real person. The characters are in danger. The players never are.

If something stops working for someone, the story adjusts. That's not a failure. That's the game functioning correctly.

NO EXPLANATION REQUIRED

When a player uses any safety tool, no one asks why. No one needs to know why. The tool is used, the table responds, and play continues or adjusts. We trust each other enough not to require justification.

The same applies to the GM. If a scene isn't going the way it should, the GM can call a pause too.

"We are here to tell a story together. The safety tools are how we make sure everyone stays in the room long enough to see how it ends."

02 // SAFETY TOOLS
■ OUT OF WORLD — ACTIVE PROTOCOLS

These are the specific safety mechanics this table uses. Everyone should read and understand all of them before Session 1. They are fast, low-friction, and never require explanation.

X
PROTOCOL ST-01 // IMMEDIATE STOP
THE X-CARD

The X-Card is a hard stop. When someone invokes it — by saying "X", typing X in chat, or physically tapping a card on the table — the current scene or content stops immediately. No discussion. No questions. The table moves on or the scene is edited.

HOW TO USE IT
Say: "X" or "X-card" aloud / type X in chat
Result: Scene stops. GM adjusts, skips, or cuts. Play resumes.
Who can use it: ANYONE at the table, including the GM
Explanation required: NO

The X-Card is not a last resort. It's a first resort. Using it earlier — before something becomes genuinely distressing — is always the right call. There is no content in this campaign that is more important than the person reaching for the X-Card.

⬚
PROTOCOL ST-02 // EXIT WITHOUT PENALTY
THE OPEN DOOR

Anyone can leave the session at any time, for any reason, without explanation. The door is always open. This is not dramatic. It is not a disruption. It is a standing permission that exists for everyone, always, before it is ever needed.

HOW TO USE IT
Say: "I need to step out" or simply leave
Result: Session pauses, continues without you, or ends — your call
Who can use it: ANYONE at the table, including the GM
Explanation required: NO

When someone steps out, the table does not speculate about why. When they return — or don't — the table picks up where it left off. We don't track who has used the door or how often. We do not make it notable. It's just a door.

If you need to step away and come back, come back. If you need to be done for the session, be done. If you want to check in after, that's welcome. None of it is required.

||
PROTOCOL ST-03 // STEP OUTSIDE THE FICTION
THE PAUSE / OOW SIGNAL

The Pause signal steps out of the fiction without stopping it entirely. Use it to check in, ask a question, clarify intent, or flag that something needs a brief conversation before continuing. This is less than an X-Card — it doesn't stop a scene, it pauses it.

HOW TO USE IT
Say: "OOW" (out of world) or "Pause" or make a T-shape with your hands
Result: Table steps outside the fiction briefly. GM or player speaks. Resume when ready.
Who can use it: ANYONE at the table, including the GM
Explanation required: BRIEF — just enough to address what needs addressing

Common uses: "OOW — I want to make sure we're all okay with where this is going before it goes there." Or: "Pause — can we talk about how my character would realistically respond to this?" Or simply: "OOW — five-minute break please."

When you return to the fiction, it helps to signal it: "Back in." This prevents confusion about whether people are speaking as themselves or as characters.

↺
PROTOCOL ST-04 // NARRATIVE EDIT
THE REWIND

The Rewind lets anyone call for an edit to something that already happened in fiction — if a scene went somewhere uncomfortable and an X-Card wasn't reached for quickly enough, or if something landed wrong in retrospect. The scene can be retroactively adjusted.

HOW TO USE IT
Say: "Can we rewind that?" or "I want to edit [scene/moment]"
Result: Table discusses briefly. GM adjusts what happened. New version is canon.
Who can use it: ANYONE at the table, including the GM
Explanation required: MINIMAL — enough to identify what needs changing

The Rewind is not about retconning for convenience. It is specifically a safety tool — use it when something didn't sit right and you wish it had gone differently. The GM makes the call on how to adjust, with input from the person who called for it.

03 // LINES & VEILS
■ OUT OF WORLD — COMPLETE BEFORE SESSION 1
LINES // CONTENT THAT DOES NOT APPEAR

A Line is content that does not enter the game at all. Not offscreen, not implied — it simply doesn't exist in this campaign. Once something is a Line for anyone at the table, it becomes a Line for the whole table. No negotiation.

You don't need to justify a Line. "I don't want that in the game" is sufficient. Add it below.

VEILS // CONTENT THAT HAPPENS OFFSCREEN

A Veil is content that can exist in the fiction but happens offscreen — it is acknowledged, perhaps referenced, but not depicted in play. "We cut away before it gets to that point."

Veils can be added at any time, including mid-campaign. If something becomes a Veil after it's been depicted, the Rewind tool can retroactively apply it.

SPIKE-SPECIFIC CONTENT NOTE

Because SPIKE deals directly with psychological distress, dissociation, and emotional crisis, some content is worth explicitly discussing even if it doesn't end up as a Line or Veil. Consider having a brief conversation about:

  • Depictions of dissociation and loss of self (SPIKE 10–11)
  • Grief and death of NPCs the party is attached to
  • Content that might mirror real-world mental health experiences
  • Body horror — SPIKE's physical symptoms at high levels
  • Manipulation, gaslighting, and institutional control (themes in the campaign)

None of these are automatically Lines or Veils. They're starting points for a conversation.

04 // TABLE CONTRACT
■ OUT OF WORLD — REVIEW AND AGREE BEFORE SESSION 1

These are the agreements this table makes before play begins. Check off each item to mark that the whole table has discussed and agreed to it. These are defaults — if any item needs adjustment for your specific group, note it in the open questions section.

■ SAFETY & COMFORT
■
Safety tools are always available. Any participant may invoke an X-Card, call a Pause, use the Open Door, or request a Rewind at any time, with no explanation required and no social penalty.
■
Lines and Veils are enforced without question. Any content that becomes a Line is removed from the game entirely, permanently, for everyone. Veiled content happens offscreen. These are not subject to in-session negotiation.
■
No one is pushed through discomfort for the story's sake. If a scene is landing wrong for a real person at this table, the scene changes. The story is not more important than the person.
■
Real-world distress is taken at face value. If someone says they need to stop or step out, we do not assume they are roleplaying. We treat it as real unless they clearly indicate otherwise.
■ PLAY NORMS
■
We play our own characters. We do not dictate other players' characters' actions, emotions, or responses. We may try to influence them in fiction. We do not override them out of fiction.
■
Player versus Player conflict is handled collaboratively. Mechanical PvP may exist. Any PvP that would significantly harm another player's character arc requires OOW conversation and agreement first.
■
We separate character from player. What happens to a character is not happening to the player. Criticism of a character's choices is not criticism of the player. We never use fiction as a vector for real-world grievances.
■
Spotlight is shared. We are attentive to who is and isn't getting screen time, and we actively create space for quieter players. The GM will help with this, but players share responsibility for it.
■
We lean into the genre. SPIKE is a psychological thriller. We embrace slow tension, unreliable information, and difficult emotional content as features of the campaign, not problems to be solved quickly.
■ GM RESPONSIBILITIES
■
The GM will not use safety tools against players. Lines, Veils, and X-Cards are player protections. The GM will not exploit knowledge of what players have flagged as difficult to target them with it.
■
The GM will telegraph difficult content. Before scenes that may escalate into challenging territory, the GM will give players a brief heads-up: "This scene may go to some dark places — everyone okay to continue?"
■
The GM will check in regularly. Brief check-ins at session end are standard. If play has been particularly heavy, a mid-session or post-scene check-in may happen. Players are encouraged to answer honestly.
■
The GM holds the Lines and Veils. It is ultimately the GM's responsibility to remember and enforce all Lines and Veils, to ensure content that should not appear does not appear, and to redirect if a scene drifts toward it.
■ LOGISTICS
■
Session length and frequency are agreed in advance. If a session needs to end early, the GM will try to reach a reasonable pause point. Players give as much notice as possible for absences.
■
Communication happens between sessions too. If something is lingering from a session — something that landed wrong, something to discuss — it is welcome to raise it between sessions rather than waiting.
■
The contract can be updated. These agreements are not static. If something needs to change as the campaign develops, any participant can raise it and the table will revisit.

06 // CHECK-IN PROTOCOL
■ OUT OF WORLD — ONGOING USE
HOW WE CHECK IN

SPIKE sessions can be emotionally heavy. Brief check-ins before, during, and after play help ensure everyone is genuinely okay — not just performing okay. The goal is to create a table where "I'm not doing great" can be said simply, without drama.

The check-in is not a debrief, a performance review, or an intervention. It is a brief, low-pressure temperature check. It takes about two minutes and it matters.

PRE-SESSION // BEFORE WE START

A quick round before the session begins. Each person answers one or two questions:

  • How are you coming in today? (A word or short phrase is fine.)
  • Anything from last session still sitting with you?
  • Any changes to Lines, Veils, or comfort levels to flag?

This does not need to be deep. "I'm good, let's play" is a valid answer. The point is that the door is open for it not to be.

POST-SESSION // BEFORE WE LOG OFF

A brief round after the session ends. Each person answers:

  • How are you leaving today?
  • Anything from this session you want to flag for the GM?
  • A rose (something that worked) and/or a thorn (something that didn't)?

Post-session check-ins also catch things that didn't quite cross the threshold for an in-session X-Card but still landed a bit wrong. Better to name it here than carry it to next session.

MID-SESSION SIGNALS

If something feels off mid-session but you don't want to fully stop, look for these signals in yourself or others:

  • Going quiet when you're usually engaged
  • Joking more than usual (deflection is a real signal)
  • Seeming to check out from the fiction
  • Short responses, shorter than normal

If you notice these in someone else, a quiet OOW check-in is always appropriate: "Hey — still good?" One question. Easy to answer. Sometimes that's all it takes.
