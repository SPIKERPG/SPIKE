01 // THE WORLD
HOW TO READ THIS SECTION

The world your characters live in and the world you actually know are two different things. This section tells you what you need to understand, including things your characters believe, and a few things they don't.

Your characters' perspective is shaped by the United Global Directorate and the story the world tells itself. Your perspective as players is broader. Hold both at once.

THE COLLAPSE

The world your characters were born into, or lived long enough to remember, collapsed. World War III was not a single catastrophe but a cascade: conventional war, a nuclear exchange over the Black Sea, engineered biological weapons, and the complete dissolution of global governance happening simultaneously.

The death toll was staggering. Infrastructure failed. Supply chains vanished. Governments dissolved or turned predatory. And in the aftermath of all that loss, something else happened that may have been as dangerous as the war itself.

The world broke emotionally. Mass grief, mass rage, mass terror. Clinicians called it SPIKE. Ordinary people called it the end of everything.

SPIKE // WHAT CITIZENS KNOW

In the years following the Collapse, suicide rates, homicidal violence, and complete psychological breakdown spread faster than any disease. Communities that had survived the war destroyed themselves from within. The emotional devastation of what humanity had just witnessed and done to itself proved, in many cases, more lethal than the conflict.

Your characters understand this. They lived through it or were born into its wake. For them, SPIKE is not a clinical term. It is the name of the thing that nearly ended everything.

THE UGD

The United Global Directorate emerged from the restructured United Nations as the world's governing body. It is not a villain in your characters' minds. It is the institution that held everything together. It ran the food programs, restored communications, administered justice, and most importantly: it commissioned the Reset.

THE RESET // THE WORLD YOUR CHARACTERS CELEBRATE

The Reset is the single most significant event in living memory. A neurological intervention, delivered globally at the beginning of Year 0, stabilized the emotional baseline of the surviving population. SPIKE ended. The violence subsided. People could think clearly again, work together, rebuild.

Your characters do not think of the Reset the way you might think of a medical procedure you've heard about. They think of it the way survivors think of a rescue. It saved them. The world celebrates the anniversary of Year 0. Children are raised understanding that without the Reset, there would be no world left to grow up in.

In Year 5, the Reset is not questioned. It is not whispered about. It is simply the foundation on which everything else rests. This is important to understand as players, your characters are not skeptics. They are, in the deepest sense, grateful.

YEAR 5 // THE PRESENT

Five years of rebuilding. The world functions. It is stable, safe, and administered. The UGD governs through regional districts. Communications have been restored. Basic needs are met. There is no open conflict.

And then, recently, a SPIKE Storm. A wave of involuntary emotional escalation across a region. People hurt. People lost. The mechanism unknown. Your team has been dispatched to investigate a pre-Reset research facility that may hold answers. You are professionals. You are stable. You are Reset. This is a routine mission.

02 // LIFE AFTER THE RESET
■ OUT OF WORLD — PLAYER GUIDANCE
THE MOST IMPORTANT THING TO UNDERSTAND

Playing a Reset character is unlike playing most RPG characters. The emotional range your character operates within is very narrow. Not because they are robots, but because the neurological floor has been raised. The lowest points of despair and the highest peaks of joy are both gone.

What remains is a kind of functional calm. Your character can solve problems, form preferences, experience mild satisfaction or mild discomfort. They can care about things and about people. But the fire, the heat of emotion, is simply absent. They don't miss it. They wouldn't recognize it as missing. This is just what it is to be alive in Year 5.

"Think of it like the difference between a lake in a storm and a lake on a still morning. Your character lives permanently on that still morning. They don't remember the storm well enough to grieve it."
— GM GUIDANCE // EMOTIONAL BASELINE POST-RESET
WHAT YOUR CHARACTER CAN FEEL
  • Mild satisfaction — a job done well, a problem solved, a good meal
  • Mild discomfort — something that doesn't sit right, a situation that feels inefficient
  • Preference — they like some things more than others, have favorite people, favorite places
  • Care — they are not indifferent to the people around them; they just don't feel that care as warmth
  • Curiosity — intellectual interest in problems, questions, unknowns
  • Purpose — they believe in their mission and in the Directorate
WHAT YOUR CHARACTER CANNOT FEEL (AT BASELINE)
  • Deep grief — loss registers, but doesn't hollow them out
  • Passionate love — affection exists, but not the ache of it
  • Euphoria or elation — good news lands, but doesn't lift them
  • Fear as physical sensation — they can assess danger without their heart seizing
  • Rage — frustration exists, but not the burning kind
  • Wonder or awe — they notice beauty without being moved by it
HOW TO PLAY EMOTIONAL FLATNESS WITHOUT BEING BORING

This is the central craft challenge of SPIKE. A character with muted emotions can still be richly interesting, because their interiority is expressed through thought, habit, and preference rather than feeling.

TIP // LEAD WITH OBSERVATION
Your character notices things. They form opinions. "This facility is poorly maintained, the UGD would not approve" is more revealing than "I feel uneasy." Let your character's values and habits do the emotional work.
TIP // PREFERENCES AS PERSONALITY
What does your character prefer? A specific brand of ration? A particular method of filing reports? Strong preferences, even mild ones, create a vivid character. These are what remain when the emotional peaks are shaved off.
TIP // CARE WITHOUT HEAT
Your character can care deeply about their team, expressing it practically rather than emotionally. They check that someone is hydrated before a long walk. They make sure the quietest person in the room got to finish their sentence. Love, post-Reset, looks like good logistics.
TIP // FRAME EMOTIONS AS COGNITION
When something bad happens, your character doesn't feel it so much as process it. "This is a suboptimal outcome" instead of "I'm devastated." "I assess this as a priority concern" instead of "I'm terrified." This isn't coldness. It's their version of coping.
TIP // THE GHOST OF FEELING
Sometimes, very faintly, something registers. Your character might pause at a piece of music and not know why. They might avoid a certain topic without understanding the avoidance. These ghost-moments are valuable. Play them as small, unexplained hesitations, not dramatic revelations.
TIP // BACKSTORY AS SUBTEXT
Your character has a past. They lost people. They did things. All of that is still in them, it just doesn't ache. Let it inform how they are without letting it spill over. The subtext is richer for being subtext.
"She looked at the photograph for a long moment. It was her sister, she was reasonably certain. She had no recollection of the feeling that accompanied that knowledge, only the knowledge itself. She set it face-down on the desk and returned to the work."

— An example of Reset interiority. Notice: recognition without grief. Decisive action. The feeling is not there, but it is not being suppressed either. It is simply absent. This is the texture of your characters' inner lives.
03 // PLAYING SPIKE
■ OUT OF WORLD — PLAYER GUIDANCE
SPIKE AS EMOTIONAL RETURN

SPIKE is not simply a damage tracker. Mechanically it penalises your rolls, but narratively, it is something far stranger and more unsettling: it is emotion coming back.

Your characters have never felt strong emotion. They have no framework for it, no language for it, no coping mechanisms for it. The Reset removed all of that, and five years of post-Reset life has given them nothing to replace it with. So when SPIKE rises and something that feels like fear begins to register, they don't know what it is. They know something is wrong. They know their readings are elevated. They do not know that what they are experiencing is called terror.

This is the core dramatic engine of SPIKE. You, as a player, know what your character is feeling. Your character only knows that their neurological baseline is deviating from normal, and that this is a medical emergency.

"Your character is not going mad. They are being returned to a human state they were never taught to inhabit."
— GM GUIDANCE // THE SPIKE EXPERIENCE

The following describes the emotional texture of each SPIKE band, and how to play it. Mechanics are in the Player's Guide. This is the experience.

0
SERENE
The Ideal Operative. Perfectly calibrated. Clean baseline. Your character is at their most functional: efficient, clear-headed, and untroubled. If they have an inner life, it runs like well-maintained equipment.
"Everything is in order. I will continue."
1–2
STABLE
Normal Operating Range. The baseline most operatives maintain in the field. Minor anomalies register and resolve without incident. Nothing worth noting.
"I notice something is slightly off. I will monitor it."
3–5
SHAKEN
The First Signal. Something is wrong, but your character cannot name it. A persistent discomfort. An unusual reluctance. Thoughts that circle back without resolution. They would not call this anxiety. They don't have that word for what this is. They call it interference. A reading worth watching.
"I am experiencing elevated readings. I should note this in my log. I will... note this. Shortly."
Roleplay: Subtle. A hesitation. A statement repeated. A small refusal that has no logical basis.
6–7
CRACKED
The Cracks Open. Fear arrives, not named as fear, but felt as a physical wrongness. The body is responding to something the mind hasn't processed. Intrusive thoughts. The beginnings of what a pre-Reset person would call dread. Things that should be logical aren't landing as logical anymore. Sadness surfaces as a vague, sourceless heaviness.
"I am... struggling to complete the assessment. The data is clear. I understand the data. I do not, I cannot, I will try again."
Roleplay: Uncharacteristic pauses. Repeating tasks unnecessarily. Avoiding certain rooms, topics, eye contact, without knowing why.
8–9
OVERLOADED
The Flood. Now the negative emotions are undeniably, physically present, and your character still has no name for them. Fear is overwhelming. Grief surfaces as a pain with no source. Anger begins to push through in flashes. None of this makes sense to them. They are a stable, Reset person. This is not possible. This is a medical event. They need to report this. They cannot make themselves pick up the communicator.
"Do not, please do not, I am fine. I am assessing. Please do not ask me again right now."
Roleplay: Difficulty with social interaction. Short, clipped responses or sudden over-explanation. Unusual rigidity or unusual compliance. Something is clearly wrong and they are trying very hard not to admit it.
10
SPIKED
Loss of Self. The mechanics take over, but before they do, understand what this is: a person experiencing every emotion they were never taught to manage, all at once, at full intensity, with no framework, no language, no coping skills. This is not a villain reveal. This is a person in crisis. The character who emerges at SPIKE 10 is not a monster. They are a human being who was never allowed to practice being one.
"I don't, I don't understand what's, why is it, stop, just, STOP,"
Roleplay: Refer to the Behavior Table (Player's Guide). Lean into the tragedy. This is not fun for the character. It can be powerful and even beautiful to play, if the table holds it well.
11
CONSUMED
The Edge. Mechanically, CONSUMED causes True Damage. Narratively, it is a person burning alive in feelings they cannot understand. If a character reaches this point, it is a scene the whole table should hold with care. Treat it as a medical emergency, because in the fiction, that's exactly what it is.

HELPING A SPIKED TEAMMATE

Mechanically, you can attempt to ground a SPIKED operative. Narratively, this is one of the most human moments the game produces.

Your character does not say "It's going to be okay." They don't know if that's true, and they don't operate in that register. Instead, they might:

  • Recite a familiar procedure. Step by step. Out loud.
  • Hand them something concrete to hold or do.
  • Remind them of a fact they know to be true.
  • Sit very still and say nothing, because stillness is what they have.

The mechanical roll determines success. The roleplay determines whether the moment is memorable.

THE EMOTIONAL FLAW DYNAMIC

Emotional Flaws are scars. Not damage, but shape. A character who develops Fear of Attachment doesn't dramatically pull away from their team. They just... become a little more efficient when working alone. They notice they are filing solo reports. They stop initiating contact.

When you lean into a flaw, you are not failing your character. You are making them richer. When you resist a flaw, you are not playing it wrong. You are watching someone try, imperfectly, to be better than what happened to them.

Both choices tell a story. Pick the one that serves the moment.

04 // THE CAMPAIGN
■ OUT OF WORLD — PLAYER GUIDANCE
GENRE // PSYCHOLOGICAL THRILLER & MYSTERY

The Reeves Trials is primarily a psychological thriller and mystery. You will uncover things. You will not always understand what you've uncovered immediately. Evidence accumulates. Threads connect slowly. The picture that emerges may not be the one you expected.

There are elements of horror, the kind that comes from implication and atmosphere, not spectacle. Things will be unsettling before they are explained. Some of them may never be fully explained. Sit with the uncertainty. That's where the game lives.

WHAT TO EXPECT
  • Investigation and exploration will be as important as combat, often more so
  • Information is currency, what you know, and what you choose to do with it, shapes the story
  • Not everything is what it appears, your characters' perspective is limited; yours as players may not be
  • SPIKE is a story engine, not just a penalty system. Let it do its work
  • There are things to find here that nobody sent you to find
PACING & TONE

Sessions will not always have clear climaxes or tidy resolutions. A session might end mid-corridor, mid-conversation, mid-realisation. This is intentional. The campaign is built around accumulation, small things that compound into something large.

The horror, when it comes, will be quiet before it is loud. Pay attention to the things that feel slightly wrong before anything feels dramatically wrong. Your characters are trained observers. So are you.

Combat exists and matters, but it is rarely the answer to what is actually happening. The game rewards people who ask questions.

PLAYER VS. CHARACTER KNOWLEDGE

There will be moments where you, as a player, understand something your character does not. This is a feature, not a problem. The dramatic tension of knowing something your character can't fully articulate, of watching them almost grasp something and miss it, is one of the most powerful tools available to you.

Do not rush to give your character your understanding. Let them earn it at their own pace. The gap between what you know and what they know is where the story happens.

05 // GETTING THE MOST OUT OF IT
■ OUT OF WORLD — PLAYER GUIDANCE
INVEST IN YOUR CHARACTER'S ORDINARY LIFE
Give them habits. A preference for sitting with their back to a wall. A particular way they take notes. A small ritual before a mission. These details cost nothing and pay dividends, especially when SPIKE starts eroding them.
BUILD RELATIONSHIPS AT THE TABLE
The party dynamic is one of the best story engines in the game. Two characters who have a specific way of communicating, a shared shorthand, a recurring disagreement, will generate drama naturally, without anyone forcing it.
LET THINGS LAND SLOWLY
Post-Reset characters don't react immediately to emotional stimuli. When something significant happens, your character might not visibly respond in the moment, and then, three scenes later, something small tips them sideways. That delayed reaction is realistic and dramatically potent.
TAKE NOTES
The mystery is built to be solved, but only if the details are tracked. Your character sheet has space for intelligence and open questions. Use them. Things the GM mentions in passing are often important. The document your character glanced at and didn't read fully? Go back and read it.
LEAN INTO THE UNCERTAINTY
The instinct in a thriller is to resolve ambiguity as quickly as possible. Resist it. When something is strange, let it be strange for a while before your character tries to explain it away. The strangeness is information.
TRUST THE GM WITH YOUR SPIKE
The GM is not trying to break your character. SPIKE is a collaborative story tool. When something raises your SPIKE, it's an invitation into a scene, a character beat, something interesting. Say yes to the invitation.
CELEBRATE FAILURE AS MUCH AS SUCCESS
A failed roll that costs you something (a raised SPIKE, a lost resource, a new complication) generates more story than a clean success. The worst moments for your character are often the best moments for the game. Hold them with both hands.
CHECK IN WITH EACH OTHER
This is a psychologically intense game. Between sessions or even mid-session, it's good practice to check in with the people at your table, setting the characters aside for a moment. A quick "how is everyone doing?" is always appropriate. You are playing together. Take care of each other.
A NOTE ON THE RESET AS ROLEPLAY CONSTRAINT

Playing a character with limited emotional access can feel limiting at first. It isn't. It is a specific creative challenge that produces specific rewards. The moment your character's SPIKE rises and something starts to break through the flatness, when they experience something they have absolutely no category for, that is one of the most interesting things you can play.

The Reset is not a cage. It is the setup. SPIKE is the payoff. Trust the design. The game is built around that arc.
